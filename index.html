Background

Why should we care?

The use of pharmaceutical drugs is a necessary part of our modern life. Staying infection and virus free allows us to enjoy a long, healthy life. Although physicians prescribe specific doses of medication, our bodies only utilize a portion of it. The leftover medication is excreted and then sent to the local wastewater treatment plant (WWTP). Current wastewater treatment methods do not degrade or remove pharmacutical compounds or byproducts from wastewater. Until a new treatment methodology or system is in place, it is necessary to analyze effluent samples before it is sent to communities as "clean water." Wastewater effluent typically has a diverse matrix of chemicals, this makes detection and quantitation of low-level pharmaceuticals difficult. Current methods in detection of pharmacuticals utilizes remote sensors that identifies general water quality changes as a sign of presence. These are not quantitative methods and do not allow technicians to know if the water is safe to discharge. Discharged water with high amounts of pharmacutical compounds can have negative health effects of humans and ecosystems exposed. To solve this issue we need a way to quantitatively analyze wastewater effluent for pharmaceutical compounds and mitigate martix interferences. Additionally, WWTP technicians are the primary handelers of the final effluent and it is likely they do not have access to a chemical laboratory or the time to learn these intriquite quantitative methods. That is why we offer an easy to use kit of reagents and preprogrammed instrumentation method to analyze a group of common pharmaceutical drugs.

Springfield, Ohio

SAL-WWTP
(Spring-A-Leak)

The SAL-WWTP receives water form the local town of Springfield which has a population of ~30 - 50,000. There has been evidence of a variety of antibiotics contaminating the WWTP. With insufficient methods of removal the town has become severely jaundiced.

Whoville, Christmas

Christmas WWTP

The town of Whoville is very remote and receives all of its drinking water from the Christmas WWTP. The local popultion utilizes antidiabetic medication due to their increased sugar diet. The WWTP is unable to removed these compounds and has caused all citizens to be affected by anosmia. In the higher altitudes of town there is even evidence of skin discoloration and excess hair growth.
